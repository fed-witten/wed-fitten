I was going to stop with competition problems, but I looked at the 2025 exam and saw there was a fun-looking problem that involved a coloring procedure similar to that of Problem B3 from 2022:

NoteProblem B1, 2025 Putnam Mathematical Competition

Suppose that every point in the plane is colored either red or green, subject to the following condition: For every three noncollinear points \(A,B,C\) of the same color, the center of the circle passing through \(A,B\) and \(C\) is also of this color. Prove that all points of the plane are the same color.

Unlike the previous two posts, I had not seen this problem before today, so I may or may not obtain a complete solution. I’ll simply write out my thought process with minimal editing.

Later: I think I came up with something approximating a solution here, but I’ve not proofread it carefully, and there are some places I could be more detailed.

Thinking

I will use the character 0 to represent both the real number 0 and the origin of the plane, not because it is not confusing, but because I am too lazy to go back and make edits.

My first thought was to first prove that, say, some circle centered at the origin contains e.g. only red points, then see if I could use this to show that a circle of any radius has only red points.

Suppose I succeed in showing there is some closed disk \(D_r\) with radius \(r>0\) centered at \(0\) such that all points of \(D_r\) are, say, red. Now, for \(R>r\), let \[C_R=\{(x,y)\in \RR^2:x^2+y^2=R^2\}\] be the circle of radius \(R\) centered at 0.

I claim that \(C_R\) has at most two green points. Indeed, if there were three points \(A,B,C\in C_R\) colored green, then by assumption the center of the circle passing through \(A,B,C\) would have to be green. But the circle passing through \(A,B,C\) is \(C_R\), which is centered at 0, and \(0\in D_r\) is red by assumption. Thus, \(C_R\) has at most two green points.

At this point, I thought that maybe this observation about the circles is a more promising starting point, and restarted as follows:

Suppose without loss of generality that \(0\) is colored red. For each \(R>0\) and each \(P=(p_1,p_2)\in \RR^2\) colored red, let \[C_R(P)=\{(x,y)\in \RR^2:(x-p_1)^2+(y-p_2)^2=R^2\}\] be the circle of radius \(R\) centered at P.

I claim that \(C_R(P)\) has at most two green points. Indeed, if there were three points \(A,B,C\in C_R\) colored green, then by assumption the center of the circle passing through \(A,B,C\) would have to be green. But the circle passing through \(A,B,C\) is \(C_R(P)\), which is centered at 0, which is red by assumption. Thus, \(C_R(P)\) has at most two green points for all \(R>0\).

Similarly, for every point \(Q\in \RR^2\) colored green, let \(C_r(Q)\) be the circle of radius \(r\) centered at \(Q\). Then, by the same reasoning as above, \(C_r(Q)\) has at most two red points.

Meta comment/intuition/visual argument for the final touches

Here I try to explain the intuition for the last part of the argument, but I’m not sure it makes any sense. It is a representation of how I was thinking, though, so I’ll leave it. The next section is an attempt at translating this section into mathematics.

At this point, it is clear that we must be close to being done. We know that \(0\) is a red point, and we know that every circle centered at \(0\) has at most two green points. This clearly implies that almost all points of \(\RR^2\) are red. On the other hand, if there exists any green point \(Q\) in \(\RR^2\), then the point above shows that every circle centered at \(Q\) has at most two red points, so almost all points of \(\RR^2\) are green.

Using measure theory here to derive a contradiction would be stupid and against the spirit of this kind of problem. A more elementary idea is to fix some circle centered at 0 large enough to contain \(Q\). Then, take a circle centered at \(Q\) that is small enough to be contained in the bigger circle. Slowly increase the radius of the small circle; eventually it will intersect the larger circle at one point, then for a while it will intersect at two points, then it will intersect at one point, and after that it will contain the other circle. But the circle containing \(0\) has at most two green points, so in some interval the circles centered about \(Q\) will “sweep out” a pair of arcs with only red points, and since each of these circles has at most two red points, the region that they sweep out will contain only green points. But part of this area is lying in the interior of our circle centered at \(0\), so there exists some smaller circle centered at \(0\) intersecting an infinite number of green points. But a circle centered at \(0\) has at most two green points; contradiction.

Finishing touches

Suppose there exists a point \(Q\in \RR^2\) that is colored green (so \(Q\neq 0\) since \(0\) is red), and choose \(R>0\) large enough that \(Q\) lies in the interior of the circle \(C_R(0).\) Then, by above, \(C_R(0)\) has at most two green points. Now let \(X\in C_R(0)\) be the point of \(C_R(0)\) of shortest distance from \(Q\) (such \(X\) exists and is unique since \(Q\) is in the interior of \(C_R(0)\) and \(Q\neq 0\)). For \(t\in (0,\infty)\), consider the family of circles \(C_{d+t}(Q)\). Let \(t'>0\) be the value of \(t'\) such that \(C_{d+t'}(Q)\) intersects \(C_R(0)\) at one point so that \[|C_{d+t}(Q)\cap C_R(0)|=2\] for all \(t\in (0,t')\).

Since \(C_R(0)\) has at most two green points, there exists an interval \((a,b)\subset (0,t')\) such that both points in \(C_{d+t}(Q)\cap C_R(0)\) are red for all \(t\in (a,b)\). Let \(B_R(0)\) denote the ball of radius \(R\) centered at \(0\) so that \(C_R(0)\) is its boundary. Now let \(R\) be the region \[R=\{(x,y):(x,y)\in C_{d+t}(Q)\cap B_R(0)\text{ for some }t\in (a,b)\}.\] For each \(t\in (a,b)\), the two red points of \(C_{d+t}(Q)\) both lie on \(C_R(0)\), so every point of \(R\) is green.

Now let \(\alpha=(a+b)/2\) and let \(P\in C_{d+\alpha}(Q)\cap B_R(0)\). Then there exists an \(\varepsilon>0\) such that \[B_\varepsilon(P)\subset R\subset B_R(0).\] I cannot be bothered to make this more rigorous at this point: over the interval \((a,b)\), the \(C_{d+t}(Q)\cap B_R(0)\) sweep out a green region that clearly has positive area, so clearly there is an open ball in this region that is green. Now choose a circle \(C\) centered at \(0\) such that \(C\cap B_\varepsilon(P)\) contains infinitely many points. Since all the points of \(B_\varepsilon(P)\) are green, this shows that \(C\) has infinitely many green points. But \(C\) is centered at the red point \(0\), so \(C\) has at most two green points. Contradiction.

Comments

I think this works, but I’ve not done a true proofread. In any case, there is no way this is the intended solution; I’m sure there is a shorter and clearer way of going about.
